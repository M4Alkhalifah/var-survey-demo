What is this?

My survey paper reviews 38 academic works on AI-powered Video Assistant Referee (VAR) systems published between 2021 and 2026. This page brings the paper's taxonomy to life by applying it to six famous football incidents — from Lampard's 2010 ghost goal to Argentina vs Saudi Arabia at the 2022 World Cup — and showing what each surveyed AI method would say about each one.

1
2010 World Cup · Round of 16

Frank Lampard's "Ghost Goal" — England vs Germany

What happened: Trailing 2–1, England's Frank Lampard struck the crossbar; the ball clearly bounced over the goal line by ~60 cm before spinning back out. The Uruguayan referee did not award the goal. The match ended 4–1 to Germany. Two years later, IFAB approved Goal-Line Technology — this incident is widely credited as the catalyst for the entire field of automated officiating in football.

What modern AI would say

Hawk-Eye GLT deployed since 2012
GOAL CONFIRMED

Ball fully crossed the line by 60 cm. Vibration alert to referee's watch in <0.5 s, accurate to within 5 mm.

X-VARS explanation engine

"The ball crossed the goal line in full. Award the goal. The defender's recovery does not change the fact that the ball was already in the goal." — in the style of Held et al. 2024.

99.7%

1986 World Cup · Quarter-Final

Maradona's "Hand of God" — Argentina vs England

What happened: Maradona punched the ball into the net with his left hand. Tunisian referee Ali Bin Nasser, unsighted, awarded the goal. Maradona later attributed the goal "a little with the head of Maradona, and a little with the hand of God." A retroactive case study for modern handball detection.

What modern AI would say

SoccerNet-v3D 3D pose triangulation
HANDBALL DETECTED

Triangulated 3D pose shows Maradona's left wrist contacting the ball at ~2.4 m height; head was 30 cm below contact point.

X-VARS explanation engine

"The ball was struck with the player's hand, not his head. The arm was raised above the head and made contact with the ball. Goal must be disallowed and a free kick awarded to the defending team." — in the style of Held et al. 2024.

96.3%

3
2014 La Liga · Granada vs Real Madrid

Pepe's Horror Tackle — A Foul Recognition Case

What happened: A reckless studs-up challenge by Pepe, the kind of incident that defines the hardest task in AI VAR: classifying not just that a foul occurred, but how severe it was. Severity depends on speed of approach, body-part contact, and intent — exactly what multi-view foul models try to capture.

What modern AI would say

VARS (Held 2024) multi-view foul classifier
RECKLESS · RED CARD

Classified as "Tackling" with sanction "Red card" using 4-view MViT-v2 backbone — consistent with VARS reporting 50.0% foul-type accuracy on similar high-severity incidents.

Foul-Pose (Goka 2025) pose-based predictor

"High-velocity approach detected 0.8 s before contact. Studs-up posture inferred from skeletal keypoints. Pre-emptive flag: high foul probability." — pose-based pre-emptive prediction.

MatchVision (Rao 2024) foundation model fine-tune
87.9%

4
2018 World Cup · France vs Australia

The First-Ever VAR Penalty in a World Cup — Griezmann

What happened: Antoine Griezmann was tripped by Joshua Risdon in the box. The on-field referee waved play on. After consulting the VAR screen, he reversed his decision and awarded a penalty — the first VAR-assisted penalty in World Cup history. France went on to win the tournament. This incident demonstrated the upstream task: action spotting (when did contact occur?) feeding the downstream foul classifier.

What modern AI would say

ASTRA (Xarles 2024) action spotting transformer
INCIDENT FLAGGED · t = 58:36

Audio-visual fusion identifies a high-impact contact moment in the penalty area; flagged for VAR review automatically. Average-mAP 66.8% on SoccerNet.

X-VARS explanation engine

"Defender's leading foot makes contact with the attacker's standing leg before the ball. Contact occurs inside the penalty area. Recommend penalty kick." — in the style of Held et al. 2024.

78.1%

5
2020 Premier League · Liverpool vs Aston Villa

The Armpit Offside — A VAR Millimetre Decision

What happened: Sadio Mané ruled offside by his armpit, by less than a centimetre. VAR took 90+ seconds to decide; the still-image-based system drew lines that appeared to flicker between frames. Sparked the global debate: is millimetre-precision offside actually fair? Directly motivated SAOT.

What modern AI would say

Real-Time Offside (Desai 2025) single-camera
OFFSIDE · 0.7 cm

YOLOv8 + MediaPipe pose + vanishing-point calibration produces a verdict in <1 s at 30 fps from a single broadcast feed.

AutoExplain (Rahimian 2025) natural-language reasoning

"At the moment of the pass, the attacker's leading shoulder is 0.7 cm beyond the second-last defender's torso. The decision is technically offside, but the margin is below human visual perception threshold." — the kind of explanation referees and fans need.

Time to decision SAOT vs old VAR
25 s

SAOT cuts decision time from ~70 s (VAR) to ~25 s.

2022 World Cup · Group C · Argentina vs Saudi Arabia

SAOT's Big Moment — Three Argentina Goals Disallowed

What happened: Saudi Arabia famously beat Argentina 2–1 — one of the great World Cup upsets — and Semi-Automated Offside Technology (SAOT) had its biggest day yet, disallowing three Argentine goals for tight offsides. The system used 12 stadium cameras tracking 29 body points per player at 50 fps, plus a sensor inside the official Adidas Al Rihla ball. It is the academic reference deployment for every paper in this survey's Offside & SAOT category.

What modern AI would say

FIFA SAOT 12-camera Hawk-Eye system
3× OFFSIDE

29 body points/player × 50 fps × 12 cameras + 500 Hz inertial-sensor ball. Decision pipeline ~25 s.

MatchVision (Rao 2024) foundation model

"All three Argentina attackers crossed the second-last defender's leading body part by 4–7 cm at the moment of the through-ball. Decisions consistent with the official call." — the foundation model approach now leading SoccerNet leaderboards.

Confidence 3D triangulation
99.2%

About this demo

What this is. An illustrative companion to my survey paper "AI-Powered Video Assistant Referee (VAR) Systems: A Critical Review and Assessment of the State-of-the-Art". The paper reviews 38 open-access academic works (mostly 2021–2026) and proposes a 7-category taxonomy of AI-VAR methods.

What this is not. The "AI verdicts" are not real-time inferences from running models. They are illustrative outputs based on the published behaviour of each cited method on similar incidents. Reproducing real inferences would require GPU compute, model weights, and synchronised multi-view footage that match each model's training distribution — out of scope for a static showcase.

Why I built it. To give Dr. Sultan, classmates, and any visitor a quick, interactive way to understand what the survey is actually about. Reading a 9-page paper is a commitment. Watching one short clip and seeing what the AI would say takes 30 seconds.
